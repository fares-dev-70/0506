Country guide

🇮🇹 Italy

Italy requires a two-step process: recognition by the Ministry of Health (Ministero della Salute), then enrolment with the provincial TSRM-PSTRP professional order. You must also pass an Italian language exam before you can practise.

Track 1 — Professional recognition
Submit dossier to the Ministero della Salute — DGPROF Ufficio 04 (Viale Giorgio Ribotta 5, 00144 Roma). Filed by post. No online system for non-EU applicants.
Track 2 — Work visa
Employer obtains a Nulla Osta through the Decreto Flussi quota system, then you apply for a Type D visa at the Italian consulate in Egypt.
Track 1 — DGPROF recognition (step by step)
1
Prepare your dossier
Fill in Application Form D2-26 (Fisioterapista – paese extracomunitario), download from salute.gov.it. Affix a €16 bollo fiscale (revenue stamp). Include: degree certificate legalised by Italian consulate in Egypt or via apostille chain; official academic transcript; detailed syllabus; clinical placement records; translation into Italian by a sworn translator certified by the Italian consulate in Cairo or authenticated by the Italian tribunal; photocopy of passport (certified by Italian consulate or municipal office – colour copies NOT accepted). A Certificate of Good Standing from the Egyptian professional body is also advisable.
⚠ Egypt is NOT in the Hague Convention. Documents must be legalised by the Italian Diplomatic/Consular Authority in Egypt — not through a simple apostille. Plan 6–10 weeks for this chain.
2
Mail the dossier to DGPROF in Rome
Send by registered post (raccomandata A/R) to: Direzione Generale delle Professioni Sanitarie e delle Politiche in favore del SSN — DGPROF Ufficio 2, Viale Giorgio Ribotta 5, 00144 Roma, Italy. Keep the receipt. Allow several months before any response — the backlog is real and well-documented.
3
Wait for the Ministry decree — 12–24 months
The Ministry may request additional documents at any point (interrupting the clock). Possible outcomes: full recognition decree (equipollenza); conditional decree requiring an aptitude test or supervised placement (tirocinio); or rejection. The backlog for non-EU health professionals is significant — 12–24 months is realistic, sometimes longer.
⚠ You cannot practise while waiting. There is no interim/temporary registration in Italy for non-EU applicants awaiting a decree.
4
Pass the Italian language exam
Once the decree is issued, before enrolling with the provincial Order, you must pass an Italian language exam — oral and written — to demonstrate you can communicate safely with patients and colleagues. This is conducted by the provincial TSRM-PSTRP Order. Typically requires B2 level; in practice you need genuine fluency. Certificate from a recognised Italian language institute (e.g. CELI, CILS, PLIDA) is accepted as evidence.
5
Enrol with the provincial TSRM-PSTRP Order (Albo)
After the Ministry decree and language clearance, register with the TSRM-PSTRP Order in the province where you will work (e.g. tsrmpstrproma.it for Rome). Submit the Ministry decree, proof of language, passport, and pay the annual membership fee (~€150–200/yr). You are now legally registered to practise in Italy.
ℹ️ Note: professional recognition (decreto) does NOT entitle you to use the Italian academic title "Dottore in…" and does NOT allow enrolment in postgraduate programmes (master, specialisation, PhD). That requires separate academic recognition (equipollenza accademica) through a university.
Track 2 — Decree Flussi, Nulla Osta & visa
⚠ Italy's work visa system is one of the most complex in the EU. Non-EU workers are subject to annual quotas (Decreto Flussi). The 2026–2028 decree authorises ~500,000 entries over 3 years. However, quota applications open on unpredictable "click days" — windows that close in hours. This requires careful preparation and timing.
1
Find an Italian employer
The employer must be an Italian entity registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate. Healthcare employers: SSN (public), IRCCS, private clinics, rehabilitation centres. Jobs: Borsa Lavoro regionale, indeed.it, InfoJobs, direct applications to ASL (local health authority) or private clinics. The 2026 Decreto Flussi introduced a specific sub-quota for nurses and physiotherapists, allowing faster Nulla Osta processing in healthcare — a positive development for this profession.
2
Employer applies for Nulla Osta via Sportello Unico per l'Immigrazione (SUI)
The employer submits the Nulla Osta request to the provincial SUI (Prefettura) during the Decreto Flussi window. The application is online and processed on a first-come, first-served basis. The employer must confirm intent to hire within 7 days of notification. This process can take weeks to months depending on the quota sub-category.
3
Apply for Type D work visa at the Italian consulate in Egypt
Once the Nulla Osta is granted, the employer is notified and you can apply for the Visto per Lavoro Subordinato (Type D) at the Italian embassy or consulate serving Egypt. You must enter Italy within the visa validity window. Required: Nulla Osta copy, employment contract, accommodation proof, passport, financial means evidence. Visa fee: ~€50–116.
4
Arrive and sign Contratto di Soggiorno — within 8 days
Within 8 working days of arrival, you and your employer go to the SUI to sign the Contratto di Soggiorno (residence-work contract). This is mandatory — missing it can void your permit. Then apply for the Permesso di Soggiorno per Lavoro Subordinato at the local post office (Poste Italiane — specific offices handle immigration forms).
⚠ The physical Permesso di Soggiorno card may take months to issue after arrival, but you are already legally entitled to work once the Contratto di Soggiorno is signed.
5
Register with the Comune (municipal office)
Register your address at the local Anagrafe (registry office). This gives you a Certificato di Residenza — needed for many services. Apply for your Codice Fiscale (tax code) at the Agenzia delle Entrate if not already issued with your visa.

🇫🇷 France

France is the most difficult of the three for a non-EU physiotherapist. The direct route (autorisation d'exercice via DREETS) exists, but is subject to a 2% annual intake cap. The realistic alternative is completing a partial exemption programme at a French physiotherapy institute.

⚠ The 2% quota rule (Article 27–32 of the 2015 decree on the physiotherapy state diploma): The total number of non-EU candidates admitted to French physiotherapy institutes in a given year via partial exemption cannot exceed 2% of that institute's annual intake. In practice this means very limited places nationwide and often waitlists of 2–3+ years. This is the single biggest structural barrier for Egyptian applicants.
Track 1 — Professional recognition (non-EU route)
DREETS (Direction Régionale de l'Économie, de l'Emploi, du Travail et des Solidarités) in the region where you will work. Requires proof of 1,500 hours of clinical experience across 5 domains.
Track 2 — Work visa
Autorisation de travail (work authorisation) obtained by the employer, then Visa de long séjour valant titre de séjour (VLS-TS) – salaire from the French consulate in Egypt.
Track 1 — Two sub-routes depending on your clinical experience
Route A — Direct autorisation d'exercice
If you can demonstrate ≥1,500 hours of clinical experience in France covering all 5 required speciality areas (orthopedics, neurology, cardio-respiratory, pediatrics, geriatrics), you can apply directly to the regional DREETS for an autorisation d'exercice — without completing further training.

If there are gaps, the commission may require validation internships (stages de validation) in France before granting the authorisation.
  • ✓ Faster if experience is well-documented
  • ✗ Requires 1,500h verifiable hours in France across all domains
  • ✗ Egyptian experience may not fully count
Route B — Partial exemption + selection test (most common for new graduates)
Non-EU graduates without sufficient French clinical experience apply for a dispense partielle de formation at a French physiotherapy institute. Your file is assessed; if admitted, you complete the missing portions of the French curriculum and then sit the state diploma exam (Diplôme d'État de Masseur-Kinésithérapeute).

Subject to the 2% annual intake cap per institute.
  • ✓ Leads to the French state diploma — full equivalence
  • ✗ Very limited places (2% cap)
  • ✗ Could take 1–3 years on a student visa
  • ✗ Costs: tuition €175–250/yr + living costs
1
Identify your target region and DREETS
Contact the DREETS in the region where you plan to work. Each region manages its own dossier process and commission dates (CRAE — Commission Régionale d'Autorisation d'Exercice). Some regions now require online dossier submission only (e.g. Occitanie from June 2025). Do not send paper dossiers to a wrong region.
2
Submit your dossier to DREETS
Required documents: degree certificate (legalised by French consulate in Egypt — same legalisation chain as Italy since Egypt is not in Hague Convention); official transcript; detailed programme certificate; academic grade certificate; thesis/dissertation summary; employment certificates documenting clinical experience by domain; proof of French language proficiency (minimum B2 strongly advised; C1 for professional practice); identity document; project of establishment in that region (you must have a clear plan to work there).
💡 The DREETS requires a clear projet d'exercice — you need to show a genuine connection to the region (job offer, accommodation, local ties). Without this, many dossiers are returned.
3
Commission CRAE assessment — 6–24 months
The regional commission examines your file and compares your training to French standards. Possible outcomes: full autorisation d'exercice; compensatory measure required (aptitude test or supervised placement); admission to a physiotherapy institute for partial exemption training; or rejection. The commission meets only a few times per year, creating long delays.
4
Register with the Ordre des Masseurs-Kinésithérapeutes
Once the autorisation d'exercice is granted, register with the regional Conseil de l'Ordre des Masseurs-Kinésithérapeutes. Mandatory to practise in any capacity (salaried or liberal). Pay the annual membership fee (~€75–150/yr). You receive your carte professionnelle.
5
Affiliate with the Assurance Maladie (CPAM)
To treat patients under the French health insurance system and bill the Sécurité Sociale, register with your local Caisse Primaire d'Assurance Maladie (CPAM). In private practice, you also need a SIRET number (business registration). For salaried positions, the employer handles this.
Track 2 — Work authorisation & visa
1
Secure a job offer from a French employer
Public hospitals (AP-HP, CHU, CHR), private clinics, EHPAD (elderly care), rehabilitation centres, sports clubs. Job boards: ANFH, Pôle Emploi Santé, LinkedIn, indeed.fr, direct hospital applications. Physiotherapy is in shortage in rural areas and geriatric settings — these are easiest to enter as a non-EU applicant.
2
Employer applies for Autorisation de Travail
The employer (or you, for self-employment) applies to the DREETS for an autorisation de travail. For salaried workers, the employer files on the Services-Entreprises platform (formerly GRETA). France generally requires a labour market test (employers must show no suitable EU candidate was available), though this may be waived for healthcare shortage roles. Processing: 1–3 months.
3
Apply for the long-stay visa (VLS-TS) from Egypt
Apply at the French consulate in Cairo for a Visa de Long Séjour valant Titre de Séjour — Salarié (VLS-TS salarié). Duration typically 1 year, renewable. Required: autorisation de travail, employment contract, CV, degree (legalised), accommodation proof, financial means. Processing: 3–8 weeks. Visa fee: ~€99.
4
Arrive in France and validate your visa
Within 3 months of arrival, validate your VLS-TS on the ANEF portal (Administration Numérique pour les Étrangers en France) and pay the tax: €200 for salaried workers. Register with the CPAM, open a French bank account, register your address at the mairie, apply for a Carte Vitale (health card). After 1 year, apply to renew your residence permit as a Titre de Séjour Salarié.

🇧🇪 Belgium

Belgium has the most structured and transparent recognition process of the three, with a clear maximum 4-month decision timeline once your file is complete. However, the language requirement (Dutch in Flanders, French in Wallonia/Brussels) is strict, and the highly-qualified salary threshold for the Single Permit is higher than a typical entry physio salary.

ℹ️ Belgium's linguistic complexity: Belgium is split into three linguistic regions. If you work in Flanders (Dutch-speaking), recognition goes through the Flemish Department of Care (Departement Zorg). In Wallonia or Brussels (French-speaking), it goes through the Fédération Wallonie-Bruxelles (FWB). You must choose your region before applying and work in that region. The process in Brussels can go through either system depending on your employer.
Track 1 — Recognition (non-EU, outside EEA)
ℹ️ For non-EEA applicants (Egypt qualifies), Belgium requires three sequential steps before you can practise: NARIC equivalence → Department of Care/FWB recognition → FPS Health visa. Then the INAMI/RIZIV number.
1
Step 1A — NARIC academic equivalence
Flanders: Apply to NARIC Vlaanderen (naricvlaanderen.be) for equivalence of your basic physiotherapy diploma. This confirms your Egyptian degree is at the correct academic level (bachelor's equivalent). Fee: €90. Processing: up to 4 months.
Wallonia/Brussels: Apply to the Service Équivalences de la Fédération Wallonie-Bruxelles (equivalences.cfwb.be). Fee: €14–150 depending on procedure. Processing: 3–6 months.
2
Step 1B — Professional recognition by Department of Care / FWB
Flanders: Submit recognition application to Departement Zorg (departementzorg.be) at kinesitherapeuten@vlaanderen.be. Submit one PDF including: completed recognition form, NARIC equivalence decision, degree certificate, transcripts, clinical placement documents, passport. All documents must be translated into Dutch by a sworn translator. Processing: maximum 4 months from complete file.
Wallonia/Brussels: Similar process through the Direction Générale Opérationnelle Santé (DGO Santé Wallonie).
⚠ If there are significant differences between your Egyptian training and Belgian standards, the Recognition Committee may require an aptitude test or a supervised adaptation period (stage d'adaptation) before granting recognition.
3
Step 1C — FPS Health visa (visa INAMI/RIZIV)
This is a specific Belgian requirement for healthcare practitioners. After receiving the Department of Care recognition, apply to the Federal Public Service (FPS) Health, Food Chain Safety and Environment for the visa to practise — this is a professional authorisation, not an immigration visa. Submit both the NARIC equivalence AND the Department of Care recognition to the FPS Health. This step often takes an additional 3–6 months as it goes through multiple committee reviews.
4
Step 1D — INAMI/RIZIV number (billing/insurance number)
Once the FPS Health visa is obtained and you are employed, apply for your INAMI number (French: Institut National d'Assurance Maladie-Invalidité) or RIZIV number (Dutch). This is your professional billing number — required for all patient billing within the Belgian health insurance system. Apply through your employer or directly via INAMI. This number is what allows your patients to be reimbursed for sessions.
Based on real experience: the full process from start (NARIC application) to receiving an INAMI number took approximately 9–10 months for one EU physiotherapist (US-trained) in 2021. For a non-EU Egyptian applicant, budget 12–18 months.
Track 2 — Single Permit (Permis Unique / Gecombineerde Vergunning)
⚠ Critical salary threshold issue: Belgium's Single Permit for highly qualified workers requires a minimum gross annual salary of €48,912 (Flanders, 2026 pending update) or €53,220 (Wallonia) or €3,703/month (Brussels). Entry-level physiotherapy salaries in Belgium typically start at €32,000–38,000 gross — below the highly-qualified threshold. This means most entry-level physio roles will NOT qualify for the highly-qualified Single Permit. However, Belgium also allows Single Permits under a standard labour market test route — this requires the employer to prove no suitable EU candidate was available. Healthcare shortage roles may benefit from an exemption or expedited labour market test.
1
Employer submits Single Permit application to regional authority
Flanders: DWSE (Departement Werk en Sociale Economie) — online portal since 2024.
Wallonia: SPW (Service Public de Wallonie) — online from 2025.
Brussels: Actiris/Brupartners.
The employer is the applicant. Two sequential approvals needed: regional (labour market) + federal Immigration Office (residence). Total processing: typically 4–6 months.
2
Apply for Type D entry visa at Belgian consulate in Egypt
Once the Single Permit is approved, apply for a Type D long-stay visa (Visa D) at the Belgian consulate or embassy serving Egypt. Documents: Single Permit decision, employment contract, passport, health insurance proof, accommodation evidence, financial means. Fee: ~€180. Processing: 3–6 weeks typically.
3
Arrive and register with the commune — within 8 days
Register at your local commune's Aliens Department within 8 days of arrival. You will receive a professional residence card (A card for workers). This is required before you can register with the professional order, work in a hospital, or obtain your INAMI number. Open a Belgian bank account (BNP Paribas Fortis, KBC, ING, or Belfius are main options) and register with the CPAS (if applicable) for social services.
4
After 5 years — permanent residence
After 5 years of legal residence with continuous work permits, you can apply for an EU long-term residence permit, which gives you permanent right to live and work in Belgium and, with certain conditions, across the EU. Belgian citizenship requires a longer period and additional conditions.

Common to all three countries

Egypt document preparation — the critical foundation

This step is common to Italy, France and Belgium and takes the longest. Egypt is NOT a member of the Hague Apostille Convention, which means every document needs a full consular legalisation chain — not just a simple apostille stamp.

⚠ No Apostille for Egyptian documents. The Hague Apostille Convention (allowing a single authentication stamp) does not apply to Egypt. Every document destined for Italy, France or Belgium must go through: 1) Egyptian Notary → 2) Egyptian Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA) stamp → 3) The target country's embassy/consulate in Cairo legalises it. Allow 6–10 weeks and significant cost. Use a professional legalisation agent in Cairo if possible.

Action plan

Universal checklist — start here regardless of country

These steps apply whichever country you choose and should be started immediately, in parallel.

🗂 Phase 1 — Do now, from Egypt (months 0–3)
1.Choose your target country and language. The language requirement is non-negotiable. If you already speak French (common in educated Egyptian families), France or Belgium (Wallonia/Brussels) becomes more accessible. If you are starting from zero, Italian or French are easier for Arabic speakers than Dutch.
2.Start language lessons immediately. Enrol in Italian/French/Dutch classes in Egypt — Alliance Française Cairo, Italian Cultural Institute Cairo, or certified online programmes. You need B2 at minimum, C1 is better. This takes 12–18 months of study even with talent.
3.Request your university documents immediately. Full transcripts in English + Arabic, subject-by-subject syllabus, clinical placement records (hours per speciality). These can take 4–8 weeks from Egyptian universities.
4.Obtain Certificate of Good Standing from the Egyptian Physiotherapy Syndicate and a police clearance certificate from the Ministry of Interior.
5.Begin the Egyptian consular legalisation chain for each document: Egyptian notary → Ministry of Foreign Affairs → target country's consulate/embassy in Cairo. Allow 6–10 weeks per batch and budget €150–300 per document set.
6.Commission certified translations into the target language from a sworn translator accredited by the target country's consulate. Italy: into Italian. France/Belgium-Wallonia: into French. Belgium-Flanders: into Dutch.
🗂 Phase 2 — Recognition applications (months 3–18+)
7.Italy: Submit dossier by registered post to DGPROF Rome (form D2-26). France: Contact target region DREETS and submit online or by post. Belgium: Apply to NARIC (Flanders) or FWB (Wallonia) for academic equivalence first, then Departement Zorg / DGO Santé.
8.Start job hunting in parallel — do not wait for recognition to finish. Build a profile on LinkedIn (set location to your target country), contact hospitals and clinics directly, use local job boards (indeed.it, jobs.be, poleemploi.fr). In all three countries, physiotherapy shortages exist especially in rural areas and geriatric/elderly care settings.
9.Pass the language exam (CELI/CILS for Italian, DELF/TCF for French, NT2 for Dutch) ideally before your recognition is complete — so there is no extra delay after the decree/decision arrives.
🗂 Phase 3 — Permit, visa & arrival (months 12–24)
10.Once a job offer is confirmed, employer applies for the relevant work authorisation: Italy (Nulla Osta via SUI/Decreto Flussi), France (Autorisation de Travail via DREETS), Belgium (Single Permit via regional authority).
11.Apply for the entry visa at the consulate in Cairo: Italy (Type D — lavoro subordinato), France (VLS-TS salarié), Belgium (Type D long-stay).
12.Save your relocation fund. Recommended minimum before first salary: Italy €5,000–8,000 / France €4,000–7,000 / Belgium €5,000–8,000.
13.On arrival: within 8 days register at commune/municipality, sign any required employment contract supplements, begin residence permit or Permesso di Soggiorno / Titre de Séjour / Verblijfskaart process.
