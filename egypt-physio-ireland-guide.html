Track 1 · Professional Recognition

CORU qualification recognition

Done entirely from Egypt. CORU is Ireland's health regulator. They compare your Egyptian degree against Irish Standards of Proficiency before you can legally practise.

1
Gather and certify all documents
Start this before anything else — it takes the longest.
  • Degree certificate (certified/notarised copy)
  • Full academic transcripts with subjects and grades
  • Detailed course syllabus from your university — subject by subject
  • Clinical placement logbook with hours per speciality
  • Certificate of Good Standing from the Egyptian Physiotherapy Syndicate
  • Passport (notarised copy)
  • CV and full employment history
  • Two professional reference letters (supervisors or clinical heads)
⚠ Egypt is not a signatory to the Hague Apostille Convention — documents require full consular legalisation (notarisation → Ministry of Foreign Affairs → Irish Embassy in Cairo). Allow 4–6 weeks and budget €80–200 per document set.
2
Create account on recognition.coru.ie
Start your application on CORU's online portal. Upload all documents section by section: qualifications, placements, internships, employment history. Check your inbox weekly — CORU may request additional documents at any point.
💡 Name files clearly: Passport_Notarised.pdf, Transcript_CairoUniversity.pdf, PlacementLog.pdf. Messy uploads cause delays of weeks.
3
Pay the €410 recognition fee
Paid online at the end of the application at recognition.coru.ie. Keep your receipt — you must include a copy with your file. Without payment, the file is not processed. This fee is non-refundable.
4
Wait for the assessment — up to 4 months
The Physiotherapists Registration Board compares your qualification against Ireland's Standards of Proficiency. The clock starts once your file is complete — not when you submit. If documents are missing, the timer pauses.
⚠ Do not book flights or sign contracts that depend on this outcome — approval is not guaranteed and cannot be rushed.
5
Two possible outcomes
CORU assesses your qualification and will either grant full recognition or require a compensatory measure.
✅ Full recognition
Best case
Your Egyptian degree is deemed equivalent. You receive a Letter of Recognition. Proceed to CORU registration. You can now legally practise in Ireland.
  • ✓ Can register immediately
  • ✓ No additional time in Ireland before working
  • ✓ Clean path to work permit
⚠️ Period of Adaptation required
Harder path
CORU identifies gaps in training. You must complete 6–12 months of supervised practice in Ireland before full registration. Creates a visa problem — see the POA section.
  • ✗ No specific POA visa exists for non-EU nationals
  • ✗ Must find workaround (aptitude test / employer / student visa)
6
Complete CORU registration
After receiving your Letter of Recognition, apply for registration on the CORU register. Pay the annual registration fee (~€380/year). You are now a CORU-registered physiotherapist — legally allowed to treat patients in Ireland. You can also join the Irish Society of Chartered Physiotherapists (ISCP) as a full member.
✓ Contact for queries: registration@coru.ie  ·  Portal: recognition.coru.ie
Track 2 · Immigration

Critical Skills Employment Permit + visa

Physiotherapy is on Ireland's Critical Skills Occupations List — this removes the most burdensome requirement (Labour Market Needs Test) and gives employers every reason to hire internationally.

What being on the Critical Skills list actually means for you
An employer can hire you directly without first advertising the job in Ireland and the EU to prove no local candidate exists. This is a huge practical advantage — it takes weeks off the employer's process and makes them far more willing to commit to an overseas hire. What it does NOT do: you still need a named employer, they still apply on your behalf, and you are still tied to their role and salary.
1
Find a job offer from an Irish employer
This is the prerequisite for the permit. The employer must be registered with Revenue, trading in Ireland, and registered with the Companies Registration Office. Their workforce must be at least 50% EEA nationals.
Where to look: HSE Careers (hse.ie/careers) · ISCP job board (iscp.ie) · LinkedIn · Irish physio recruitment agencies · direct approach to private MSK/sports clinics
💡 Many Irish employers will issue a conditional job offer pending CORU recognition — this is normal and accepted. Mention early in your application that your CORU process is underway.
2
Employer applies for Critical Skills Employment Permit via EPOS
The employer submits through Ireland's Employment Permits Online System (EPOS — launched April 2025). The employer is the official applicant and pays the €1,000 fee. Key eligibility checks:
  • Salary ≥ €40,904/year gross (from 1 March 2026)
  • Role is on the Critical Skills Occupations List (physiotherapy ✓)
  • Employer has 50%+ EEA employees
  • Permit applied for ≥ 12 weeks before start date
⚠ If your employer refuses or cannot pay the permit fee, you can pay it yourself — either the employer or employee can be the applicant. If refused, 90% (€900) is refunded.
3
Apply for the Irish employment visa from Egypt
Once the permit is approved, apply for a D-category long-stay employment visa through VFS Global in Egypt (the Irish embassy in Cairo processes via Abu Dhabi). Apply up to 3 months before travel. Required documents include: permit approval letter, employer letter on headed paper, signed contract, CV, passport (all pages), bank statements, passport photos.
📍 Irish visa application centre in Egypt: VFS Global (Cairo)  ·  Visa fee: ~€60–100  ·  Processing: 4–8 weeks
4
Travel to Ireland & register with immigration (IRP card)
On arrival, register with the Garda National Immigration Bureau (GNIB) within 90 days to get your Irish Residence Permit (IRP card). Fee: €300. This is your legal right to live and work in Ireland. You receive a Stamp 1 in your passport.
5
After 2 years → apply for Stamp 4
Stamp 4 gives you long-term residency. You can work without a permit, change employers freely, and stay up to 5 years. After 5 years total residence you can apply for permanent residency. After meeting further requirements, Irish citizenship.
✓ Spouse and dependants can join you immediately on a Dependent Employment Permit — they can work freely in Ireland without needing their own Critical Skills Permit.
6
If you lose your job
If made redundant, contact employment.permits@enterprise.gov.ie immediately. You have 6 months to find a new employer and apply for a new permit before you must leave Ireland. The Critical Skills Permit is tied to the specific employer — you must apply for a new one if you change jobs (until you have Stamp 4).
⚠ There is no formal grace period — your permit becomes technically invalid immediately on leaving that employer. Act fast.
Special scenario

If CORU requires a Period of Adaptation

A POA means CORU found differences between Egyptian and Irish physiotherapy training. You must complete 6–12 months of supervised practice in Ireland before full registration. The problem: there is no POA-specific visa for non-EU nationals.

⚠ The Irish Embassy cannot issue a visa specifically for a Period of Adaptation. This is a documented gap in the system that affects non-EU applicants from countries like Egypt, Pakistan, Nigeria, and India. You have three workarounds.
Option A — Request the Aptitude Test instead of POA
Recommended first
If the embassy cannot grant a visa for POA purposes, you can ask CORU to substitute the POA with an Aptitude Test — a written and practical competency exam. This can be sat in Ireland during a short tourist visit or, in some formats, arranged remotely. Contact CORU directly to request this option; they must agree to it.
  • ✓ No need to live in Ireland for 6–12 months before registration
  • ✓ Cleanest, fastest route if CORU agrees
  • ✓ Short tourist visit to Ireland may suffice for the exam
  • ✗ Exam pressure — requires solid preparation
  • ✗ CORU must agree — not automatic
Option B — Secure an employer willing to bring you over first
Strong option
If an Irish employer is willing to hire you conditionally (knowing CORU registration is pending), they can apply for a Critical Skills Employment Permit before your registration is finalised. You arrive on a work permit, and the POA is completed as part of your employment. The ISCP offers temporary membership for people in exactly this situation, making the arrangement recognisable to employers.
  • ✓ Arrives in Ireland with fully valid legal status (work permit)
  • ✓ POA is likely to be paid — you're an employee
  • ✓ Most natural transition to full employment after POA
  • ✗ Hard to find an employer willing to commit before CORU is finalised
  • ✗ Employer takes a risk — POA outcome is not guaranteed
Option C — Student visa + Irish master's programme
Longer path
Enrol in an Irish university master's programme (MSc Physiotherapy or a related health science), obtain a student visa, and complete the POA simultaneously during your studies. Student visas allow part-time work up to 20 hours per week during term. After graduating with an NFQ Level 9 qualification, you can stay on a Graduate Stamp (Stamp 1G) for up to 24 months to seek employment.
  • ✓ Established, well-understood visa route for Egyptians
  • ✓ Adds a postgraduate qualification — boosts job prospects and salary
  • ✓ Post-study work rights for up to 24 months after graduation
  • ✗ Expensive: tuition (€12,000–20,000+) plus living costs for 1–2 years
  • ✗ Adds 1–2 years to the overall timeline before you start working fully
  • ⚠ Must coordinate with the university to allow POA during the programme
💡 Is the POA paid? There is no universal rule — it depends entirely on the host employer or clinic. Some hospitals pay a salary during POA because they intend to keep you afterwards. Others treat it as an unpaid supervised placement. Option B gives the best chance of a paid POA since you are officially their employee.

This makes HSE the natural route for international applicants as it satisfies both CORU and CSEP requirements simultaneously.
Month by month

Realistic full timeline

Based on getting full recognition (no POA). If POA is required, add 6–18 months depending on which route you take.

Month 0 — Start immediately, from Egypt
Documents first. Request full syllabus and clinical placement records from your Egyptian university — this takes weeks. Get degree, transcripts, and passport notarised. Apply for Certificate of Good Standing from the Egyptian Physiotherapy Syndicate. Commission certified translations if needed.
Month 1–2 — Submit CORU + start job hunting in parallel
Open account at recognition.coru.ie. Upload all documents, pay the €410 fee. Keep checking your CORU inbox weekly. Simultaneously: apply for physiotherapy jobs on HSE Careers, ISCP job board, LinkedIn. Contact Irish physio recruitment agencies. Frame your application around your English-medium training — it's a genuine advantage.
Month 2–4 — Secure a conditional job offer
Ideally negotiate a conditional job offer pending CORU recognition — Irish employers hiring from Critical Skills categories know this process. Employer initiates the Critical Skills Employment Permit application via EPOS. Permit processing: 5–6 weeks.
Month 3–5 — CORU decision arrives
Best case: Letter of Recognition received — immediately start your CORU registration application (separate step). Worst case: Period of Adaptation required — refer to the POA section. Do not book flights until you have the Letter of Recognition in hand.
Month 4–5 — Permit approved
Once the Critical Skills Employment Permit is approved (5–6 weeks from submission), you can immediately apply for the Irish employment visa from Egypt through VFS Global. Gather all visa documents: permit letter, employer letter, contract, passport, bank statements.
Month 5–6 — Visa issued, book flights
Visa processing from Egypt: 4–8 weeks typically. Once visa is stamped, book flights and temporary accommodation. Research the rental market in your target city (daft.ie is the main Irish rental platform). Start looking for a permanent flat — the housing market is very competitive; begin before you arrive.
Month 6–7 — Arrive in Ireland
Land in Ireland. Week 1 priorities: temporary accommodation, SIM card, begin PPSN application (MyWelfare.ie). Within 90 days: register with GNIB for IRP card (€300). Within 2 weeks: open a bank account (bring PPSN + proof of address + passport). Finalise CORU registration if not already complete. Start work.
Month 7 onwards — Building stability
Work under the Critical Skills Permit. Spouse and family can apply to join. Annual increments increase your HSE salary each year. After 2 years: apply for Stamp 4 (long-term residency, full labour market access). After 5 years residence: permanent residency. Eventually: Irish citizenship.
If POA required — add 6–18 months before this timeline
Request the Aptitude Test (Option A — fastest), or find an employer to bring you under a work permit for the POA period (Option B), or enrol in a master's programme on a student visa (Option C). Each route adds time and cost; see the POA section for full details.
🗂 Quick-start checklist — what to do first
1. Contact your Egyptian university and request: full English-language transcripts, subject-by-subject syllabus, and clinical placement records (hours per speciality).
2. Request a Certificate of Good Standing from the Egyptian Physiotherapy Syndicate.
3. Get your degree and passport notarised, then go through the full Egypt consular legalisation chain (MFA stamp → Irish Embassy stamp).
4. Create your account at recognition.coru.ie and begin uploading documents. Pay the €410 fee.
5. Start applying for Irish physiotherapy jobs in parallel — don't wait for CORU to finish. Use hse.ie/careers, iscp.ie job board, LinkedIn and recruitment agencies.
6. Save a minimum of €8,000–12,000 as your relocation fund to cover all fees, deposits, and first-month living costs before your first Irish salary arrives.
7. Once you have a job offer, ask the employer to begin the Critical Skills Employment Permit application via EPOS immediately — processing takes 5–6 weeks minimum.
